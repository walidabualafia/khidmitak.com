The Story

Fatima Al-Masri comes from a long line of soap makers in Nablus, where the tradition of Nabulsi soap dates back over a thousand years. Her family's workshop has been producing soap using the same methods since the 13th century.

"When I was a little girl, I would watch my grandmother and mother make soap in the traditional way - mixing olive oil with water and qilw (a mixture of sodium compounds from the Dead Sea). The process takes nearly three months from start to finish, with each batch needing to cure in special drying rooms," Fatima recalls.

Today, Fatima maintains these ancient techniques while innovating with new scents like bay leaf and chamomile. Her soaps are still cut by hand and stamped with her family's seal.

Cultural Significance

Nabulsi soap is more than just a cleansing product - it's a symbol of Palestinian heritage and resilience. The soap's distinctive rectangular shape and golden color have remained unchanged for centuries, making it one of the oldest continuously produced soaps in the world.
